WILLIAM BLAKE'S CANTERBURY PILGRIMS

 

Painting, Engraving, Rivals, Contemporary Commentary

 

Introduction: William Blake’s Canterbury Pilgrims

 

[illus. BB36A.1, adv. to Exhibition] In May of 1809, Blake mounted a one-man exhibition of nine paintings and seven watercolors in the rooms above his brother’s haberdashery shop in Soho—Blake’s childhood home—at 28 Broad Street, Golden Square. The paintings were executed in a water-miscible opaque paint of Blake’s invention, which he called “fresco,” and several of the watercolors were borrowed from his patron Thomas Butts. Among the frescos were the Spiritual form of Nelson Guiding Leviathan, the Spiritual Form of Pitt Guiding Behemoth, and Satan Calling up his Legions. The exhibition was anchored by two of its largest frescos, The Ancient Britons, described by Blake as “large as Life,” making it by far the largest painting Blake ever executed, and Sir Jeffery Chaucer and the nine and twenty Pilgrims on their journey to Canterbury [illus. BUT653, painting]. The former work is lost, and the latter Blake discusses for 27 of the 68 pages comprising the exhibition’s Descriptive Catalogue. The catalogue contains what Charles Lamb called “a most spirited criticism of Chaucer … mystical and full of Vision.”1 The painting, which once belonged to Butts, is now in the Stirling Maxwell Collection at Pollock House, Glasgow. It is commonly dated 1808 (the date is obscured), and, at 46.7 x 137 cm, is among Blake’s largest works.

 

[illus. BUT654, drawing] From the start, Blake intended to reproduce his painting as an engraving. He executed a reduced pencil drawing of the design (35.4 x 95.8 cm) for the purpose, transferring it to the copper plate and announcing in a prospectus, dated 15 May 1809, that it would “be 3-feet 1-inch long, by 1-foot high” and available “One Year from September next,” noting that “No Work of Art, can take longer than a Year: it may be worked backwards and forwards without end, and last a Man's whole Life; but he will, at length, only be forced to bring it back to what it was, and it will be worse than it was at the end of the first Twelve Months.” [illus. BB12A, adv. for engraving] Blake goes on to say that “The Value of this Artist's Year is the Criterion of Society: and as it is valued, so does Society flourish or decay.” The price “to Subscribers” was “Four Guineas, Two to be paid at the time of Subscribing, the other Two, on delivery of the Print. Subscriptions received at No. 28, Corner of Broad-street, Golden Square; where the Picture is now Exhibiting, among other Works, by the same Artist. The Price will be considerably raised to Non-subscribers.”2

 

In his prospectus, Blake places himself in the company of the “old original Engravers,” Albrecht Dürer, Lucas van Leyden, Hans Sebald Beham, and Heinrich Aldegrever, and declares that his “correct and finished Line manner of Engraving,” like that of the masters with whom he allies himself, “can delineate Character as it is in this Picture, where all the Lineaments are distinct.”3

 

[illus. Blake’s engraving] In October 1810, Blake completed the slightly smaller engraving of the painting, entitled Chaucers Canterbury Pilgrims (30.7 x 94 cm, the largest plate he ever executed), the copper plate of which went through at least three subsequent states between 1810 and Blake’s death in 1827.4 The engraving shown here is from the third state out of four states.

 

Despite Blake’s efforts and aspirations, the 1809 exhibition was a failure, as were his attempts to sell his engraving, both of which contributed to Blake’s retreat into the relative obscurity in which he was to remain until his death. The single public notice of the exhibition was Robert Hunt’s scathing review in the 17 September 1809 Examiner, wherein Blake is referred to as an “unfortunate lunatic,” and his Descriptive Catalogue described as a “farrago of nonsense.”5 [illus. Stothard’s painting] Moreover, the production of Blake’s painting and engraving of the Canterbury pilgrims coincided with the highly popular painting of the same subject by Blake’s longtime friend, Thomas Stothard. Blake’s former publisher Robert Cromek helped promote Stothard’s painting and commissioned Luis Schiavonetti to execute the engraving of Stothard’s work [illus. Stothard’s engraving]. The controversy over who first had the idea for a work depicting the procession of the Canterbury Pilgrims – Blake, Cromek, or Stothard – remains unsettled. For his part, Blake retained a sense of betrayal to the end of his life, and the circumstances surrounding the Chaucer works ended his friendships with Stothard and Cromek.

 

What follows are three exhibits. The first describes in detail the pilgrims and miscellaneous figures and structures in Blake’s engraving [illus. Blake’s engraving].

 

The second exhibit begins with a general introduction into the controversial circumstances of rival works executed by Blake and Stothard, supplemented by Blake’s and Cromek’s own writings on the works, and compares eight figures from Blake’s and Stothard’s engravings.

 

The third exhibit showcases a selection of contemporary commentary and criticism to both Blake’s and Stothard’s designs, including Blake’s three-page analysis, Blake’s Chaucer: An Original Engraving by him from his Fresco Painting….

 

The Rivals: William Blake, Robert Cromek, and Thomas Stothard

 

[illus. BUT654 Blake’s painting] Blake’s 1809 exhibition of his painting Sir Jeffery Chaucer and the nine and twenty Pilgrims on their journey to Canterbury occurred nearly two years after his friend Thomas Stothard’s painting, The Pilgrimage to Canterbury, was presented for public display at the house of Blake’s erstwhile agent Robert Cromek, in March 1807 [illus. Stothard’s painting]. The similar subject matter begs the question: who first conceived of the idea? Given the conflicting testimony of the persons involved, and the lack of indisputable evidence, it is a question that has proven nearly impossible to resolve.

 

According to the first biographical account of Blake, included in the second volume of John Thomas Smith’s Nollekens and His Times (1829), in late 1805 Cromek saw designs sketched for Blake’s fresco of the procession to Canterbury during the time he commissioned Blake to illustrate Robert Blair’s The Grave. Cromek published his edition of The Grave with Blake’s illustrations in 1808, but he deprived Blake of the most remunerative part of the project, the engraving of the designs, and employed Luis Schiavonetti to etch and engrave the plates. Blake’s friend John Linnell, adding to Smith’s account, claimed that Cromek was so impressed by Blake’s Canterbury Pilgrims design that he commissioned him to complete the fresco, with the additional promise of a more lucrative fee for engraving it. But, Linnell continues, similar to what he had done in the case of The Grave, Cromek secretly approached William Bromley about engraving Blake’s design, and, after Blake became suspicious and withheld the design from his agent, Cromek brought the idea to Stothard without telling either painter of the other’s involvement.

 

Smith also gives Stothard’s version of the story, in which Blake visited Stothard while the latter was working on his design of the Canterbury Pilgrims (having already been approached by Cromek), and was so delighted with the work that Stothard “expressed a wish to introduce [Blake’s] portrait as one of the party, as a mark of esteem.”6 In this telling, Blake made no mention of his own work during his visit, and Stothard only learned of it in 1810, to his great surprise and indignation. Whether one believes Linnell’s (who is recalling Blake’s) or Stothard’s version of events, the affair ended the friendship between the two painters.

 

For his part, Cromek, in an April 1807 letter to the poet James Montgomery, claimed that the idea for the Canterbury Pilgrims design came to him after reading Chaucer’s work, and he gave himself “great Credit for thinking of such a glorious Subject.”7 At the time of the letter, Stothard’s painting had already been advertised in print by Cromek and been on public display at Cromek’s house. Blake, in a letter that has not survived, apparently had written to Cromek expressing frustration with the success of Stothard’s work. In reply, Cromek challenged Blake “to send [him] a better” portrayal of the pilgrims.8 In this recounting, then, Blake’s painting arose from Cromek’s taunting Blake for his dissatisfaction with Stothard’s painting and Blake’s anger at its success.

 

[illus. Blake’s engraving] As for the engravings of Blake’s work, Alexander Gilchrist, in his 1863 Life of William Blake, claims that a subscription paper for Blake’s engraving of the “Canterbury Pilgrims” was circulating in 1806,9 and Blake’s acquaintance Henry Crabb Robinson also refers to such a subscription paper in his memoirs.10 The existence of such a document would do much to discredit both Cromek’s claim that he and not Blake first had the idea for the work, and Stothard’s claim that Blake got the idea when visiting him as he was working on his own painting. [illus. BB12A, adv. for Blake’s engraving] However, the earliest extant prospectus for Blake’s engraving of his painting is dated 1809. In it Blake promises to finish his within a year, and was late by only a few months.

 

The engraving of Stothard’s painting, however, had a much more circuitous fate: after initially negotiating with Bromley, Cromek soon changed course and offered the commission to Schiavonetti, who had engraved Blake’s Grave designs. Cromek, however, returned to Bromley with the commission after Schiavonetti’s death in June 1810. Cromek announced in print that he would “labour jointly” with Bromley on the etchings Schiavonetti had commenced, yet this plan proved unsuccessful, as Cromek employed another engraver, Francis Engleheart, to work on the plate in September 1810. The engraving was still not finished at the time of Cromek’s death in March 1812, whereupon his widow engaged Schiavonetti’s brother Niccolo to complete the project. However, Niccolo died in 1813, with the plate still unfinished. It wasn’t until October 1817 that the engraving of Stothard’s painting [illus. Stothard’s engraving] was completed, by James Heath and his assistant W. H. Worthington, over ten years after the painting had first been exhibited.11

 

Regardless of whether Blake visited Stothard while the latter was working on his painting, Blake certainly demonstrates awareness of it, and of Cromek’s detailed and admiring description in his prospectus for an engraving of Stothard’s painting. Ironically, this prospectus, which also contained John Hoppner’s laudatory statements about Stothard’s work, appears in the appendix of Cromek’s 1808 edition of Blair’s The Grave, which contained Blake’s designs. In his Descriptive Catalogue for his own 1809 public exhibition, for which his Canterbury Pilgrims painting was the centerpiece, Blake references Hoppner’s praise and declares, referring to Stothard’s career as a popular commercial illustrator, “When men cannot read they should not pretend to paint. To be sure Chaucer is a little difficult to him who has only blundered over novels and catchpenny trifles of booksellers. Yet a little pains ought to be taken even by the ignorant and weak.”12 Though Blake concedes that the dress of Stothard’s figures “is correct according to authentic monuments,”13 his characters are referred to as “dumb dollies” and a “burlesque set of scare-crows.”14 Blake concludes his description of his painting in the Descriptive Catalogue with a spirited six-page critique, supplemented by quotations from Chaucer, of Stothard’s painting as described in Cromek’s prospectus.

 

Public admiration and critical favor overwhelmingly favored Stothard’s work, despite Blake’s energetic efforts to promote himself as a “poetic visionary” whose painting and engraving best represented Chaucer’s immortal work, in contrast to the “dull” painting of his “adversary,” Stothard.15 Robert Hunt, in the only public notice of Blake’s exhibit, wrote in the 17 September 1809 Examiner, “One of the pictures represents Chaucer’s Pilgrims, and is in every respect a striking contrast to the admirable picture of the same subject by Mr. Stothard, from which an exquisite print is forthcome [sic] from the hand of Schiavonetti.”16 After the failure of his exhibition, Blake withdrew from the public eye, and continued to nurse feelings of betrayal and anger toward both Cromek and Stothard until the end of his life.

 

Brief Introductory Text to Contemporary Comments

 

While the immediate popular and critical response favored Stothard's version of Chaucer’s pilgrims over Blake's, an emerging appreciation for the unique vision of Blake's design can be discerned in the decades following Blake's 1809 exhibition. Several observers assessed Blake’s engraving as befitting the age of Chaucer; some saw this quality as an unappealing 'imitation of the arts in their degraded state,' while for others it was a sign of Blake’s 'genius kindred to that of Chaucer.' Here we survey critical comments and reviews--both published and from private correspondence--from the artists’ contemporaries, who were attentive to the aesthetic rivalry initiated by the two designs
