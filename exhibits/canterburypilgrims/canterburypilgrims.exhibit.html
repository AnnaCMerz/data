The Engraving

 

The centerpiece of Blake’s May 1809 exhibition of sixteen paintings, held at his brother’s house at 28 Broad Street, Golden Square, was Sir Jeffery Chaucer and the nine and twenty Pilgrims on their journey to Canterbury. In his Descriptive Catalogue of the exhibition, Blake devotes to the painting 27 of the catalogue’s 68 pages, a passage that includes what Charles Lamb later called “a most spirited criticism of Chaucer … mystical and full of Vision.” The painting, now in the Stirling Maxwell Collection of the Pollock House in Glasgow, is commonly dated 1808 (the date is now obscured), and, at 46.7 x 137 cm, is among Blake’s largest works.

 

In October 1810, Blake completed the slightly smaller (30.7 x 94 cm, the largest plate he ever executed) engraving of the painting, entitled Chaucers Canterbury Pilgrims, the copper plate of which went through at least three subsequent states between 1810 and Blake’s death in 1827 (see Essick, Separate Plates, 60-74, for further details concerning the variations of these states). In his 15 May 1809 Prospectus for the engraving, Blake places himself in the company of the “old original Engravers,” Albrecht Dürer, Lucas van Leyden, Hans Sebald Beham, and Heinrich Aldegrever, and declares that his “correct and finished Line manner of Engraving,” like that of the masters with whom he allies himself, “can delineate Character as it is in this Picture, where all the Lineaments are distinct.” The engraving shown here is from the third state of the plate.

 

Despite Blake’s efforts and aspirations, the 1809 exhibition was a failure, as were his attempts to sell his engraving, both of which contributed to Blake’s retreat into the relative obscurity in which he was to remain until his death. The single public notice of the exhibition was Robert Hunt’s scathing review in the 17 September 1809 Examiner, wherein Blake is referred to as an “unfortunate lunatic,” and his Descriptive Catalogue described as a “farrago of nonsense.” Moreover, the production of Blake’s painting and engraving of the Canterbury pilgrims coincided with the highly popular painting of the same subject by Blake’s longtime friend, Thomas Stothard. Blake’s former agent Robert Cromek helped promote Stothard’s painting and commissioned Luis Schiavonetti to execute the engraving of Stothard’s work. The controversy over who first had the idea for a work depicting the procession of the Canterbury Pilgrims – Blake, Cromek, or Stothard – remains unsettled. For his part, Blake retained a sense of betrayal to the end of his life, and the circumstances surrounding the Chaucer works ended his friendships with Stothard and Cromek.

 

Of the two exhibits available here, the first allows viewers a detailed tour of Blake’s engraving, supplemented by formal analysis and selections of Blake’s own writings on Chaucer. The second exhibit explicitly compares and contrasts Blake’s engraving with Stothard’s painting and the subsequent engraving based on it (not completed until 1817), and provides a fuller account of the controversial circumstances of the production of the rival works, as well as selections of contemporary commentary and criticism.

The Rivals: William Blake, Robert Cromek, and Thomas Stothard

 

Blake’s 1809 exhibition of his painting Sir Jeffery Chaucer and the nine and twenty Pilgrims on their journey to Canterbury, occurred nearly two years after his friend Thomas Stothard’s painting, The Pilgrimage to Canterbury, was presented for public display at the house of Blake’s erstwhile agent Robert Cromek, in March 1807. The similar subject matter begs the question: who first conceived of the idea? Given the conflicting testimony of the persons involved, and the lack of indisputable evidence, it is a question that has proven nearly impossible to resolve.

 

According to the first biographical account of Blake, included in John Thomas Smith’s 1828 Nollekens and His Times, in late 1805 Cromek saw designs sketched for Blake’s fresco of the procession to Canterbury during the time he commissioned Blake to illustrate Robert Blair’s The Grave – a commission he would later retract and deliver to Luis Schiavonetti to complete the engravings of Blake’s designs. Blake’s friend John Linnell, adding to Smith’s account, claimed that Cromek was so impressed by Blake’s Canterbury Pilgrims design that he commissioned him to complete the fresco, with the additional promise of a more lucrative fee for engraving it. But, Linnell continues, similar to what he had done in the case of The Grave, Cromek secretly approached William Bromley about engraving Blake’s design, and, after Blake became suspicious and withheld the design from his agent, Cromek brought the idea to Stothard without telling either painter of the other’s involvement.

 

Smith also gives Stothard’s version of the story, in which Blake visited Stothard while the latter was working on his design of the Canterbury Pilgrims (having already been approached by Cromek), and was so delighted with the work that Stothard “expressed a wish to introduce [Blake’s] portrait as one of the party, as a mark of esteem.” In this telling, Blake made no mention of his own work during his visit, and Stothard only learned of it in 1810, to his great surprise and indignation. Whether one believes Linnell’s (who is recalling Blake’s) or Stothard’s version of events, the affair ended the friendship between the two painters.

 

For his part, Cromek, in an April 1807 letter to the poet James Montgomery, claimed that the idea for the Canterbury Pilgrims design came to him after reading Chaucer’s work, and he gave himself “great Credit for thinking of such a glorious Subject.” At the time of the letter, Stothard’s painting had already been advertised in print by Cromek and been on public display at Cromek’s house. A month later, Cromek wrote to Blake, who had apparently written to him – in a letter that has not survived – expressing his frustration with the success of Stothard’s work, challenging Blake “to send [Cromek] a better.” In this recounting, then, Blake’s painting arose from Cromek’s taunting Blake for his dissatisfaction with Stothard’s painting and Blake’s anger at its success.

 

As for the engravings of Blake’s work, Alexander Gilchrist, in his 1863 Life of William Blake, claims that a subscription paper for Blake’s engraving of the “Canterbury Pilgrims” was circulating in 1806, and Blake’s acquaintance Henry Crabb Robinson also refers to such a subscription paper in his memoirs. The existence of such a paper would do much to discredit both Cromek’s claim that he and not Blake first had the idea for the work, and Stothard’s claim that Blake got the idea when visiting him as he was working on his own painting. However, the earliest extant prospectus for Blake’s engraving of his painting is dated 1809. In it Blake promised to finish his within a year, and was late by only a few months. An extant drawing – shown here in two parts (part 1, part 2) – of Blake’s design, most likely used in translating the painting to the slightly smaller engraving, is dated 1809 or 1810.

 

The engraving of Stothard’s painting, however, had a much more circuitous fate: after initially negotiating with Bromley, Stothard soon changed course and offered the commission to Schiavonetti, who had engraved Blake’s Grave designs. Cromek, however, returned to Bromley with the commission after Schiavonetti’s death in June 1810. Cromek announced in print that he would “labour jointly” with Bromley on the etchings Schiavonetti had commenced, yet this plan proved unsuccessful, as Cromek employed another engraver, Francis Engleheart, to work on the plate in September 1810. The engraving was still not finished at the time of Cromek’s death in March 1812, whereupon his first wife engaged Schiavonetti’s brother Niccolo to complete the project. However, Niccolo died in 1813, with the plate still unfinished. It wasn’t until October 1817 that the engraving of Stothard’s painting was completed, by James Heath and his assistant W. H. Worthington, over ten years after the painting had first been exhibited.

 

Regardless of whether Blake visited Stothard while the latter was working on his painting, Blake certainly demonstrates awareness of it, and of Cromek’s detailed and admiring description in his prospectus for an engraving of Stothard’s painting. Ironically, this prospectus, which also contained John Hoppner’s laudatory statements about Stothard’s work, appeared in the appendix of Cromek’s 1808 edition of Blair’s The Grave, which contained Blake’s designs. In his Descriptive Catalogue for his own 1809 public exhibition, for which his Canterbury Pilgrims painting was the centerpiece, Blake references Hoppner’s praise and declares, referring to Stothard’s career as a popular commercial illustrator, “When men cannot read they should not pretend to paint. To be sure Chaucer is a little difficult to him who has only blundered over novels and catchpenny trifles of booksellers. Yet a little pains ought to be taken even by the ignorant and weak.” Though Blake concedes that the dress of Stothard’s figures “is correct according to authentic monuments,” his characters are referred to as “dumb dollies” and a “burlesque set of scare-crows.” Blake concludes his description of his painting in the Descriptive Catalogue with a spirited six-page critique, supplemented by quotes from Chaucer, of Stothard’s painting as described in Cromek’s prospectus.

 

Public admiration and critical favor overwhelmingly favored Stothard’s work, despite Blake’s energetic efforts to promote himself as a “poetic visionary” whose painting and engraving best represented Chaucer’s immortal work, in contrast to the “dull” painting of his “adversary,” Stothard. Robert Hunt, in the only public notice of Blake’s exhibit, wrote in the 17 September 1809 Examiner, “One of the pictures represents Chaucer’s Pilgrims, and is in every respect a striking contrast to the admirable picture of the same subject by Mr. Stothard, from which an exquisite print is forthcome from the hand of Schiavonetti.” After the failure of his exhibition, Blake withdrew from the public eye, and continued to nurse feelings of betrayal and anger toward both Cromek and Stothard until the end of his life.

 

In this exhibit, viewers can compare Blake’s and Stothard’s works in great detail. These images are supplemented by Blake’s and Cromek’s own writings on the works, as well as by critical responses from contemporaries and others writing in the first half of the 19th century.

 

 

 

 

 

Critical Responses:

 

Francis Douce, from Illustrations of Shakespeare and of Ancient Manners (1807): “Mr. Stothard, with every claim to superior merit, has recently finished a Painting of the Procession of Chaucer’s Canterbury Pilgrims, which may be classed among the choicest morsels of its kind. The attention to accuracy of costume which it displays, has never been exceeded, and but very seldom so well directed.” Yet according to Mertz, Douce demonstrated his preference for Blake’s design by purchasing a print of Blake’s engraving and not one of Stothard’s design.

 

Cromek’s 1808 prospectus: “These tales are the most pleasing of Chaucer’s works. It is the characters which are described in the general Prologue to them which Mr. Stothard has now transferred to the Canvas; and with so much truth and sprightliness, and in a manner so agreeable, that the Poet’s humor may, with truth, be said to be revived in the Painter. It is the particular merit of this piece, that the story is immediately brought home to the Spectator. He becomes instantly one of the group, and sees them move before him, marked by their distinctive habits, characters, and sensations, in the same manner as Chaucer has drawn them. The idea of the Poet is impressed at the first view,—a humor unforced, agreeable, and comic; a pleasurable Tour; sanctified by the name of Pilgrimage. The covert ridicule on these eccentric excursions, which Chaucer intended, is very happily preserved in his face; the quiet indifference of one of the Monks, the hypocrisy of another, and the real piety of a third, are with equal excellency portrayed. The gay levity of the Wife of Bath, and the countenance of the old Ploughman, worn down with age and labour, are finely rendered. The Miller is an admirable character; and his Horse is as much in character as himself. The Fop of Chaucer’s Age is exhibited as making a display of his riding; and the Sea Captain bestrides his Nag with the usual awkwardness of the Sailor. The pale and studious countenance of the Oxford Scholar; the stateliness of the Lady-Abbess; the facetiousness and homely humour of the Host, as contrasted with the Segeant at Law, and the Doctor of Physic;—all these peculiarities of character are very finely and delicately expressed. The costume of each Person is correct with an antiquarian exactness; and the whole Group is so well distributed, that each character is sufficiently seen, and in his due place.”

 

William Paulet Carey’s Critical Description of Chaucer’s Pilgrims to Canterbury (1808) [provide link] contains a detailed and admiring description of Stothard’s painting. “Connoisseurs talk only of the beauties of the composition, and of the triumph of the Artist.”

 

John Hoppner, from a 10 May 1807 letter to Richard Cumberland, quoted in Cromek’s prospectus and in Carey’s Critical Description: “The features of each individual are touched with the most happy discrimination of character, and prove the Painter to have studied the human heart with as much attention, and not less successfully, than [sic] the Poet. This intelligent group is rendered still more interesting by the charm of colouring, which, though simple, is strong, and most harmoniously distributed throughout the picture.” The painting “is wholly free from that vice which painters term manner” and “bears no mark of the period in which it was painted, but might very well pass for the work of some able artist of the time of Chaucer.” It has “a primitive simplicity and the total absence of all affectation, either of colour or penciling.”

 

Bentley quotes a 20 June 1810 letter from Robert Bowyer, bookseller, to Lord Earl Spencer: “Mr. Bowyer begs leave to acquaint Lord Spencer that the Plate of which the enclosed is a prospectus is Engraving in the line manner & of which W: B: has already a very fine Etching & he [Bowyer] has reason to believe it will be one of the finest Engravings which has been seen in this country for some Years.” (BR 300)

 

Bentley quotes the only known review – which appeared in the June 1812 The Lady’s Monthly Museum – of Blake’s painting as exhibited in the Associated Painters in Water Colours: “Sir Geoffrey Chaucer and the Pilgrims, by Blake is a picture of mongrel excellence; yet has such a repulsive appearance, that we doubt most of our fair readers will scarcely view it with pleasure, unless they should be well acquainted with Aunciente tapestrye, to enter into its merits. That it is the work of genius, no one will deny; it possesses all the truth, the costume, and manners of the times; and the artist is perhaps worthy of the highest commendation for his industry, research, and correctness; but for our parts, we feel ourselves so perfectly satisfied with the same subject, as treated by Stothard, that we wish not to possess a picture whose greatest merit seems to be an imitation of the arts in their degraded state.” (BR 313-314)

 

Bentley quotes Thomas Frognall Didbin, an “enthusiastic bibliographer,” who wrote in 1816: “When Blake entered the arena with Stothard, as a rival in depicting the Dramatis Personae of Chaucer’s Canterbury Tales, he seems to have absolutely lost his wits; his pencil was as inferior to that of the former, as his burin was to that of Cromek [i.e., Schiavonetti], who engraved Stothard’s immortal picture.” (BR 328)

 

Bentley quotes Charles’s Lamb’s 15 May 1824 letter to Bernard Barton: “His Pictures, one in particular the Canterbury Pilgrims (far above Stothard’s)[,] have great merit, but hard, dry, yet with grace. He has written a Catalogue of them, with a most spirited criticism on Chaucer, but mystical and full of Vision.” (BR 394)

 

Bentley quotes J. T. Smith, from Nollekens and His Times (1828): “I entertain a high respect for the talents of both persons concerned.” (BR 613)

 

Bentley quotes Allan Cunningham, from Lives of British Painters (1830): “The chief attraction [of Blake’s 1809 exhibition] was the Canterbury Pilgrimage, not indeed from its excellence, but from the circumstance of its origin, which was well known about town, and pointedly alluded to in the catalogue. The picture is a failure. Blake was too great a visionary for dealing with such literal wantons as the Wife of Bath and her jolly companions. The natural flesh and blood of Chaucer prevailed against him. He gives grossness of body for grossness of mind,—tries to be merry and wicked—and in vain.” (BR 644)

 

Bentley quotes Barron Field, from the conclusion of an anonymous and unpublished review of Chaucer Modernized, ed. Thomas Powell & R.H. Horne (1830): “Have any of our readers ever seen the print of the Canterbury pilgrims by William Blake, the mad artist, whose biography may be read in Mr. Allan Cunningham’s Lives? He painted it, he says, before Mr. Stothard’s picture on the same subject, and nearly thirty years ago he exhibited it publicly, in opposition to that elegant composition. Mr. Stothard’s picture is Chaucer modernized—the horses are all barbs, from the Elgin Marbles, and the riders, refined and softened, in stage-dresses. Mr. Blake’s picture is Chaucer in the old rough verse—“in his habit as he lived”—bad in drawing, but true and vivid in expression. His horses are wooden toys, and his dogs pigs; but the individual character is in every human face, and look and dress. Mr. Blake understood Chaucer, like Mr. Wordsworth, profoundly. Mr. Stothard read him like Mr. Horne, superficially. Together with his Exhibition, Mr. Blake gave you, for yr. shilling, a printed description of his Pictures, in contrast with Mr. Stothards’. This contained a little madness about painting, and a great deal of abuse of Mr. Stothard; but it was prefaced by some Observations on Chaucer’s Poem, of so striking and original a kind, that, as the pamphlet is now as good as MS. (few people being in the habit of preserving their Exhibition Catalogues) we shall conclude this article by quoting them. [quotes from DC] Poor William Blake! He was certainly a man of a genius kindred to that of Chaucer.” (BR 534-535)

 

Bentley quotes from Anon., “On the Genius of Stothard, and the Character of his Works”, Arnolds Magazine of the Fine Arts, NS, III (March 1834), 440: Blake’s engraving “might in its character and execution have passed for a performance or work of Art, contemporary with the times of Chaucer.” (BR 228n)
